The Case for the Vertical Stack

Why owning both the silicon and the software is the only durable moat in the age of AI, and why the margin arithmetic of fragmented supply chains is finally breaking down.

By Dhritiman Mallick · Founder & CEO, Vyuhaa Med Data
March 2026 · 9 min read

Something seismic is happening in the laptop industry, and it has nothing to do with specs. Two machines launched within weeks of each other: a $600 MacBook Neo and a $6,000 MacBook Pro with an M5 Max chip. Together they are making an argument so loudly that even rival executives are calling it "a shock to the industry." The argument is not about clock speeds or pixel counts. It is about what happens when a single company controls the entire stack: the chip, the operating system, the industrial design, and the ecosystem of services wrapped around all of it. In the age of AI, that vertical integration is becoming the most durable competitive advantage in technology. And at Vyuhaa, where we build both the hardware and the software for cancer diagnostics, we have been living this thesis every single day.

The Coordination Problem

Consider the new Dell XPS 14. By most measures it is an excellent machine: a gorgeous tandem OLED display, a thin aluminium body, Intel's latest Panther Lake silicon, three Thunderbolt ports, and a massive force touch trackpad. At $2,200 it is a premium laptop that feels premium in the hand.

But for this machine to actually deliver a premium experience, a small miracle of coordination has to occur. Dell needs to nail the hardware design and not cut corners. Intel needs this particular generation of chips, with their new integrated graphics, to be genuinely good, because Dell does not even offer a discrete GPU option. And Microsoft needs Windows to be worthy of the price tag: clean, responsive, and free of friction.

That is three independent companies, each with their own incentive structures, product roadmaps, and quality bars, all needing to peak at the same time on the same product. And lately, one of those links, the software, has been breaking under its own weight.

The Margin Stack: Where Your Money Actually Goes

There is a structural problem with the fragmented model that goes deeper than coordination. It is margin arithmetic. Every company in the supply chain needs to make a profit on its component, and those margins stack on top of one another. The customer ends up paying a compounded premium not for a better product, but simply to keep multiple corporations solvent.

Illustrative breakdown. Actual margins vary by vendor and generation, but the structural point holds: every additional company in the chain adds a margin layer the customer pays for.

When Apple builds the same class of product, it captures the silicon margin, the OS margin, and the hardware margin in a single P&L. There is no Windows license fee. There is no separate chip vendor extracting a premium for R&D recovery. The total margin can be equal to or even higher than the fragmented model, yet the product can still cost less or deliver more at the same price, because there is only one profit requirement instead of three.

This is not just a theoretical advantage. It shows up in the actual pricing. The M5 Max MacBook Pro delivers GPU performance exceeding the $10,000 M2 Ultra Mac Pro. The $600 MacBook Neo matches or beats $1,000 Windows laptops on performance per dollar. The margin stack is not a rounding error. It is a structural tax that every fragmented product pays and every vertically integrated product avoids.

The Compounding Effect

Each vendor in the chain does not just add a flat fee. They add a percentage margin on their cost base, which already includes the margins of their own suppliers. The result is compounding: the customer pays margin on top of margin on top of margin. Vertical integration collapses this into a single margin event.

The Software Tax on Premium Hardware

The margin problem is made worse when the software layer actively degrades the hardware experience. The first boot on that $2,200 XPS reportedly takes 45 minutes: forced updates, mandatory sign ins, prompts for Microsoft 365, OneDrive, Copilot. Then come the ads. A popup for McAfee Security on a brand new two thousand dollar machine. A mandatory Copilot key on the keyboard, mandated by Microsoft for "AI PC" certification. Generative AI wedged into Microsoft Paint.

Each of these decisions was made by a different team at a different company optimising for a different KPI, and the customer absorbs the incoherence. The user is not paying $2,200 for a unified vision. They are paying $2,200 for a committee product assembled by three boardrooms with competing interests.

The hardware is designed for the software and the software is designed for the hardware. When that loop is closed within a single company, everything feels efficient and clean and performant. When it is not, every seam shows.

Why AI Makes This Existential

Artificial intelligence is not just another feature. It is a workload that touches every layer of the computing stack, from how transistors are arranged on a die to how the operating system schedules inference jobs to how the user interface presents results. When these layers are controlled by different companies, AI becomes an accessory bolted on after the fact. When they are controlled by one company, AI becomes the bloodstream of the product.

Apple's neural engine is not a separate card you can buy. It is woven into the silicon that ships in every Mac, iPad, and iPhone. The operating system knows exactly how to offload work to it. The developer tools are built around it. This is why on device AI features can run efficiently on a $600 MacBook Neo: nobody had to negotiate which chip, which driver, or which API to use. The whole stack was designed as one thing.

On the Windows side, Microsoft is trying to drive an AI agenda (Copilot, Recall, NPU requirements) across hardware made by dozens of OEMs using chips from Intel, AMD, and Qualcomm. The result is not a coherent AI experience. It is a policy mandate disguised as a product. A dedicated Copilot key on every keyboard does not make the AI better. It just makes the keyboard worse.

Fragmented Model

Three Margins, Three Agendas

Chip vendor, OS vendor, and OEM each optimise locally and extract their own margin. AI features arrive as afterthoughts bolted onto existing products: a Copilot button here, a generative paint tool there. The customer pays for all three profit centres.

Vertical Stack

One Margin, One Vision

Silicon, software, and services are designed together from the start. AI workloads run on dedicated neural engines that the OS natively understands. One margin event, zero bloat, zero conflicting incentives.

The Low End Shockwave

For years, the fragmented model's saving grace was price. Apple played exclusively at the premium end. The cheapest MacBook was a thousand dollars. Windows OEMs owned everything below that line. Budget laptops like the $550 Acer Aspire 16 thrived by offering just the right set of trade offs: a bigger screen, more ports, acceptable performance, and a price tag students and families could stomach.

The MacBook Neo just detonated that comfortable arrangement. At $600, or $500 with a student discount, it offers the same metal build quality, the same keyboard, the same clean operating system, and the same tight vertical integration as machines costing twice as much. It runs on a variant of the A18 Pro, which Apple can produce at extraordinary scale and low marginal cost because it shares a production line with hundreds of millions of iPhones.

And here the margin arithmetic becomes devastating. A Windows OEM building a $600 laptop must source a $150 chip (on which Intel or Qualcomm takes a margin), a $30+ Windows license (on which Microsoft takes a margin), and still leave enough room for their own margin on the remaining $420 worth of materials, assembly, and distribution. Apple builds the chip itself, writes the OS itself, and captures all three margin layers internally. It can price the Neo at $600 and still make money, or even choose to subsidise the hardware because the real revenue comes from services.

The Trojan Horse Strategy

Apple is not primarily chasing hardware margins on the Neo. It is acquiring new Mac users who will subscribe to iCloud, Apple TV+, Apple Care, and the broader services ecosystem. The hardware is the customer acquisition cost for a recurring revenue stream. Budget Windows OEMs, who survive on thin hardware margins alone with no services flywheel, simply cannot replicate this playbook.

The early data supports the thesis. Apple's CEO recently noted that the Mac just had its best launch ever for first time customers. That is not a product metric. It is a market structure metric. Every one of those buyers is a customer that a Dell, an HP, or a Lenovo lost, not because those companies built bad hardware, but because they are structurally incapable of competing with a vertically integrated opponent who has turned hardware into a loss leader for services.

The Lesson Beyond Laptops

The laptop market is a microcosm of a much larger pattern. In every domain where AI is becoming central, the companies that control both the hardware and the software are pulling ahead. NVIDIA does not just make GPUs; it makes CUDA, the software framework that makes those GPUs useful. Tesla does not just make cars; it makes the neural networks, the training infrastructure, and the custom chip that runs them.

We see the same dynamic playing out in healthcare. At Vyuhaa, we build CerviAI, an edge AI system for cervical cancer screening. Early on we made the deliberate choice to own the full stack: the scanner hardware, the slide preparation chemistry, the inference software, and the cloud platform that ties it all together. We did this not because we wanted to be a hardware company. We did it because we learned that when you rely on a third party scanner with a third party staining protocol running a third party operating system, every integration seam becomes a point of failure, a source of margin leakage, and a place where the final clinical output degrades. The margin stacking problem is just as real in medical devices as it is in laptops. Every intermediary adds cost without adding clinical value.

The age of AI is not an age of algorithms alone. It is an age of systems, where the quality of the final experience depends on how well every layer, from transistor to interface, works in concert. The companies that own those layers will define the next era. The ones that rent them from three different vendors will spend that era scrambling to keep up.

The shock that the rest of the industry is feeling is not really about a $600 laptop. It is about the dawning recognition that in a world where intelligence is being embedded into every product, the architecture of your company matters as much as the architecture of your chip. When you own the full vertical stack, you collapse the margin layers, you eliminate the coordination tax, and you design every layer to amplify every other layer. That is not a luxury anymore. It is the price of admission.
